# IFRS vs. US GAAP — Key Differences Summary

## Overview

IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards) and US GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting
Principles) are the two dominant financial reporting frameworks globally. Public companies in the
US use US GAAP; most other jurisdictions (including EU, UK, Australia, Canada, and ~140 others)
use IFRS. Key differences affect comparability of financial statements across jurisdictions.

---

## 1. Revenue Recognition

### US GAAP (ASC 606) and IFRS 15
Both frameworks converged on a common five-step model in 2018:
1. Identify the contract with a customer
2. Identify the performance obligations
3. Determine the transaction price
4. Allocate the transaction price
5. Recognize revenue when (or as) a performance obligation is satisfied

**Key remaining difference**: US GAAP provides more detailed implementation guidance (SEC SAB 101
history); IFRS 15 relies more on principles-based judgment. In practice, revenue recognition under
the two frameworks is highly similar for most transactions.

---

## 2. Inventory Costing

### US GAAP
- Permits FIFO, LIFO, and weighted-average cost
- LIFO (Last In, First Out) commonly used in the US to reduce taxable income in inflationary
  environments
- Lower of cost or market (LCM) — write-down is permanent; reversal not permitted

### IFRS (IAS 2)
- **LIFO is prohibited**
- Only FIFO and weighted-average cost permitted
- Lower of cost or net realizable value — write-down may be reversed if circumstances change

**Impact**: Companies using LIFO under US GAAP report lower inventory values and lower
pre-tax income in inflationary periods. IFRS restatement to FIFO would increase inventory and
retained earnings (LIFO reserve).

---

## 3. Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E)

### US GAAP (ASC 360)
- Cost model only: PP&E carried at cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment
- Impairment: two-step test (recoverability then fair value); impairment losses are not reversed

### IFRS (IAS 16)
- **Choice of cost model or revaluation model**
- Under revaluation model, PP&E can be written up to fair value; surpluses go to Other
  Comprehensive Income (OCI) as revaluation reserve
- Impairment (IAS 36): one-step test using recoverable amount (higher of VIU or FVLCD);
  **impairment losses can be reversed** (excluding goodwill)

**Impact**: IFRS revaluation can result in significantly higher asset values and different
depreciation charges. This affects asset turnover, ROA, and D/E ratios comparisons.

---

## 4. Intangible Assets and Research & Development

### US GAAP (ASC 730, ASC 350)
- All R&D costs expensed as incurred (with exceptions for software development under ASC 350-40)
- Internally generated intangibles (brand, customer relationships) not recognized

### IFRS (IAS 38)
- Research phase: expensed as incurred
- **Development phase: capitalized** once technical and commercial feasibility demonstrated
- Specific criteria must be met (technical feasibility, intent to complete, ability to use/sell,
  probable future economic benefits, adequate resources, reliable measurement)

**Impact**: IFRS companies may report higher intangible assets and lower R&D expense in periods
of development capitalization, followed by higher amortization charges over the asset's useful
life. Affects EBIT margin comparisons between IFRS and US GAAP companies.

---

## 5. Leases

### US GAAP (ASC 842) and IFRS 16
Both frameworks generally converged: lessees recognize a right-of-use (ROU) asset and lease
liability for most leases on the balance sheet.

**Key difference**:
- US GAAP: Distinguishes between operating leases (straight-line lease expense in P&L) and
  finance leases (separate interest and amortization)
- IFRS 16: **All leases accounted for as finance leases** — front-loaded total expense vs.
  straight-line under US GAAP operating lease

**Impact**: IFRS 16 typically results in higher EBITDA (lease payments reclassified from
operating expense to depreciation + interest), lower operating income in early years of lease,
and higher reported debt.

---

## 6. Goodwill

### US GAAP (ASC 350)
- Goodwill not amortized; tested for impairment annually
- Two-step impairment: Step 1 (qualitative or quantitative screen) + Step 2 (measure impairment)
- Optional simplified approach: carry goodwill at reporting unit level

### IFRS (IFRS 3, IAS 36)
- Goodwill not amortized; tested for impairment annually
- One-step test: compare carrying amount to recoverable amount
- IASB is evaluating reintroduction of goodwill amortization — proposals pending as of 2024

**Current difference**: Impairment testing methodology differs. US GAAP impairment may be
triggered at a different threshold.

---

## 7. Financial Statement Presentation

### Statement of Comprehensive Income
- US GAAP: Can present as one or two statements; OCI included
- IFRS: Must present OCI; more flexibility in ordering

### Cash Flow Statement
| Item | US GAAP | IFRS |
|---|---|---|
| Interest paid | Operating | Operating or Financing |
| Interest received | Operating | Operating or Investing |
| Dividends paid | Financing | Operating or Financing |
| Dividends received | Operating | Operating or Investing |

These classification differences can significantly affect operating cash flow comparisons.

---

## 8. Summary Impact on Key Ratios

| Ratio | Effect of IFRS vs. US GAAP |
|---|---|
| Gross margin | May differ if inventory method changes (LIFO → FIFO) |
| EBITDA | Higher under IFRS 16 (lease reclassification) |
| Operating margin | Lower under IFRS 16 in early lease years |
| ROA | Differs with PP&E revaluation (IFRS higher asset base) |
| D/E ratio | Higher under IFRS 16 (higher lease liabilities on balance sheet) |
| R&D as % of revenue | Lower under IFRS if development costs capitalized |
| EPS | Affected by R&D capitalization, lease treatment, impairment differences |
